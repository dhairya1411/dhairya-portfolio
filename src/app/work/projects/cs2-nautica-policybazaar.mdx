## The Problem Nobody Had Named

At PolicyBazaar for Business, the Employee Benefits sales team renews corporate group health insurance policies annually. These are high-stakes conversations — a client's premium can jump 30–40% based on their employees' claims from the previous year.

When a CFO asks "why has our premium gone up?" — the sales rep needs an answer.

Before Nautica, they didn't have one.

There was no tool that gave sales reps visibility into a client's historical claims data. No claim ratios. No risk profile. No projected premium. Just a renewal number handed down by the insurer, and a rep expected to defend it.

I found this gap not through a formal brief, but by watching how the sales team actually prepared for meetings — and noticing what questions they couldn't answer.

---

## What I Defined

I documented the product requirements for **Nautica** — a Sales Insight Generation Platform built on AWS QuickSight. Here's what it needed to show:

**Incurred Claim Ratio (ICR)**
The ratio of claims paid out to premium received. An ICR above 100% means the insurer paid more than they received — the direct mathematical reason a premium increases. Sales reps needed to be able to show clients this number and explain the link.

**Loss Ratio**
A portfolio-level risk metric used to identify accounts where the insurer is overexposed and likely to hike premiums further.

**Client Claims Breakdown**
Total claims by category (hospitalization, OPD, maternity) — so reps could point to exactly which claim types drove the ratio up.

**Projected Renewal Premium**
Based on the client's historical claims trend, what premium range should they expect? This gave reps a data-grounded anchor instead of just accepting the insurer's number.

**Risk Profile Flag**
A High / Medium / Low tier per client, allowing leadership to prioritize retention efforts for at-risk accounts.

---

## The Technical Decision That Mattered Most

Standard claims aggregation would have overstated risk for clients with one-off high-value events — a cancer treatment, a major surgery.

I built SQL logic to separate **recurring claims from non-recurring ones**. A client who had one catastrophic event last year shouldn't carry that risk profile permanently into their renewal conversation. The segmentation made ICR and risk scores accurate for the ongoing relationship — not distorted by outliers.

This wasn't just a data engineering decision. It was a product judgment call: what story is the number telling, and is it the right story?

## Before and After

**Before Nautica:**
Sales rep walks into renewal meeting. CFO asks why the premium jumped. Rep says "industry trends" or "the insurer's calculation." Trust erodes. Client feels like a transaction, not a relationship.

**After Nautica:**
Sales rep walks in with a breakdown of exactly which claim categories drove the ICR from 78% to 112%. Can project what the premium would look like at different risk levels. Conversation shifts from adversarial to advisory.

## Impact

- **20+ ad-hoc analyses** delivered to leadership during the build, accelerating strategic decision-making
- First time sales reps had a structured, data-backed client profile before renewal meetings

## The Lesson

> Data that exists is not the same as data that is usable.

Every piece of information that went into Nautica already existed in PolicyBazaar's Claims MIS. The problem wasn't data availability — it was that nobody had translated raw MIS data into the form a sales rep could use in a room, in real time, with a skeptical CFO.

That translation is a product problem. And it was sitting there, unnamed, until someone looked at how people were actually preparing for meetings.
