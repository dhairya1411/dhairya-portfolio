## The Campaigns

During my time at Ambak, I planned and executed two WhatsApp campaigns targeting the full partner network. They look similar on the surface — both sent via Serri.ai, both reaching Ambak Saathi partners. But they were solving entirely different problems.

---

## Campaign 1 — Udaan 2.0 Winners Announcement

**The objective:** Announce the top performers from Ambak's partner incentive program, Udaan 2.0 — across the full partner network, not just the winners.

### Why Send It to Everyone?

A recognition campaign directed only at winners misses the larger job.

The goal isn't to notify 15 partners that they won. It's to show 5,000 partners what winning looks like — and make them want to be next. Public recognition is a retention tool for the whole network, not a notification for a few.

**Channels:** WhatsApp (Serri.ai) + Email API

The dual-channel approach wasn't redundancy for its own sake. WhatsApp is where partners live — it's the channel they use for everything. Email adds formality and archivability — something partners can screenshot and share. They serve different emotional jobs in the same message.

**What I'd measure:**
- WhatsApp delivery rate
- Email open rate
- App activity spike in the week post-announcement
- Udaan sign-up intent for the next program cycle

---

## Campaign 2 — RM Departure Notification

**The objective:** Prevent partner churn when a Relationship Manager left the company.

### The Risk This Addressed

In a partner-led model, the RM relationship is often the reason a partner stays. The RM is the partner's primary human contact at Ambak — the person they call when something breaks, when a lead needs pushing, when they need help with the app.

When that person leaves without warning, partners feel abandoned. They stop submitting leads. Some quietly move to competitors. The damage happens silently, before anyone notices.

This campaign's job was to interrupt that pattern before it started.

### The Design Principles

**Lead with acknowledgment, not bureaucracy.** The message opened by recognizing the departing RM's contribution — not with "we're writing to inform you of a staffing change."

**Create continuity, not uncertainty.** Partners need to know who their next point of contact is and how to reach them — immediately, not after they've already tried calling the old RM twice.

**WhatsApp, not email.** This is the channel partners used to communicate with their RM directly. Switching to email for this message would have felt institutional at the worst possible moment.

### What I'd measure

- Partner churn rate in 30/60/90 days post-RM departure (vs. historical RM departure baseline)
- Lead submission continuity — did partners keep generating pipeline?
- New RM adoption rate — did the relationship transfer?

---

## The Underlying Principle

Both campaigns are really about the same thing: **communicating at the right moment**.

The Udaan campaign reached partners at a moment of aspiration — when they're most open to being motivated. The departure campaign reached partners at a moment of vulnerability — when they're most at risk of quietly disconnecting.

Getting the moment right matters more than getting the message perfect. A brilliant message at the wrong moment doesn't land. A simple, honest message at the right moment does.

---

## The Constraint I Couldn't Ignore

Running these campaigns while knowing our WhatsApp delivery pipeline had systemic failures created a real tension. If the infrastructure is delivering less than 40% of what it sends, a campaign's "reach" metric is fiction.

The lesson from this wasn't "don't run campaigns." It was: **fixing infrastructure is a prerequisite for engagement strategy to work.** You can't build on a broken pipe.
