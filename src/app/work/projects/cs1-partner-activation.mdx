## The Setup

Ambak Home Loans distributes home loans through a network of independent agents and DSAs — called partners — who originate loan leads. Their primary tool is **Ambak Saathi**, a mobile app for checking CIBIL scores, accessing approved project listings, tracking earnings, and submitting leads.

In the six months before I joined, ~2,500+ partners had registered on the platform. The numbers looked active on the surface. They weren't.

---

## What Was Actually Happening

When I started digging into how partners were using the app, what I found wasn't a product problem in the traditional sense. It was a workflow problem.

Here's what the typical partner journey actually looked like:

**Step 1** — The RM creates a lead in Ambak Saathi on the partner's behalf.

**Step 2** — The partner takes that lead and goes directly to the bank they've always worked with — the one they already have a relationship and tie-up with.

**Step 3** — The partner's bank processes the loan through their existing channel.

**Step 4** — Once the loan is disbursed, the partner calls the RM and says "the case went through."

**Step 5** — The RM marks the case as disbursed in the system. Since the Ambak DSA code was used, the partner gets their commission payout.

The partner got paid. The RM fulfilled their role. The system registered activity. But the partner never independently used the app to do anything meaningful.

---

## Why This Was Hard to See

The workflow above was invisible in the data. CleverTap showed sessions, events, and feature interactions. What it couldn't show was *who* was actually behind those interactions — the RM or the partner — because there was no reliable way to distinguish between the two at the login level.

Twenty percent on the flagship CIBIL feature was a red flag. But the funnels alone couldn't explain it. That required talking to people.

## What 30–40 RM Conversations Revealed

I spoke with 30–40 Relationship Managers to understand what was happening on the ground. Three things stood out:

**Many partners simply didn't want to change how they worked.** They had existing bank tie-ups, existing relationships, and an existing workflow that got them paid. Ambak Saathi was an addition, not a replacement. The switching cost felt higher than the benefit.

**Partners who were willing to try the app didn't get enough to go on.** There was no structured onboarding — no walkthrough of what the features did, no explanation of why checking CIBIL through Ambak was better than their existing method. Partners were handed a login and expected to figure it out.

**The RM became the default interface.** When partners had questions or needed something done, they called their RM. The RM handled it in the app. This created a functional but fragile dependency — partners got outcomes without building any capability of their own.

---

## Why Proxy Detection Was Difficult

One thing I wanted to understand was whether the app activity we were seeing came from partners or from RMs acting on their behalf. This turned out to be harder than expected.

There was no reliable technical signal to separate RM-initiated sessions from partner-initiated ones at the account level. You'd need either separate login flows or explicit session tagging to track this accurately — neither of which existed.

This made the activation data fundamentally ambiguous. "Active users" could mean genuinely self-sufficient partners, or it could mean RMs keeping the numbers alive on their behalf.

---

## What I Did

**Analytics Audit**
I audited the CleverTap event mapping and found naming inconsistencies that were making funnel data unreliable. I proposed corrected event nomenclature — because you can't make decisions on data you can't trust.

**Partner Activation CRM**
The onboarding team had no visibility into which partners were genuinely engaged vs. dormant vs. never started. I defined requirements for a **Partner Activation CRM** inside Ambak Sangam that surfaces:
- Activation status per partner (Never Opened / Opened / Activated / Retained)
- Retention flags (Active / At Risk / Dormant / Churned)
- Recommended next action for the onboarding team

**Engagement Campaigns**
While the CRM was being built, I executed two partner engagement campaigns to maintain network activity — detailed in a separate case study.

---

## The Core Insight

> A partner who gets paid without using your product has no reason to start using it.

The activation problem at Ambak wasn't primarily about the app's usability. It was about whether the app fit into a workflow that partners already had — and whether anyone had made the case for why it should.

Until Ambak Saathi is more valuable to a partner than their existing bank relationship, adoption will remain optional. That's a product-market fit problem, not just an onboarding problem.

---

## What I'd Measure

- **North Star:** % of registered partners independently completing at least one core feature action per month
- **Activation rate:** % completing first self-served action within 30 days of joining
- **Feature utility rate:** % of CIBIL checks initiated by partners (not RMs) — requires session-level identity separation
- **Onboarding completion rate:** % of new partners who receive and complete a structured app walkthrough
